# Writing examples

Ten worked pieces across the situations Another Creation has to write for. The bio is real; the rest are plausibly fictional, dated, and named — so the voice can show, not be described.

Companion to `writing-guidelines.md`. When drafting something new, look here for the closest reference shape and write next to it.

---

## 1 — Article (journal piece)

*The Linen Comes from Belgium*

The flax grows on a strip of land between Bruges and the French border, where the soil and the river do work that hasn't changed in five generations of the same family. The mill is called Libeco. The linen they spin is the linen Ýr cuts.

The flax is dew-retted in the fields — left out to weather, broken down by morning damp and afternoon sun. Mechanically retted flax is faster and easier, but the fibre loses something in the process: a softness, a depth, a willingness to take a dye without going dull. Dew retting is older. It is also less reliable. The mill accepts both as part of the work.

A bolt of linen arrives at the studio in Reykjavík a few weeks before the season starts. The first thing that happens is nothing — the bolt sits, breathing, getting used to the room. Linen has memory. It remembers the air it last knew. Cut it cold and it shrinks awkwardly; cut it after a week of being in the studio, and it lies flat.

The spring coat is two and a quarter metres of this. Cut wide enough to be wound twice, loose enough to take a layer underneath. Lined in cotton, also from a small mill, this one in Como. The cotton is the lighter mate; the linen carries the shape.

The piece will be worn for years. It will fade where the body bends it — at the elbows, the inside of the wrist, the line of the breast. After ten summers it will be a different garment, marked by the woman who wore it. This is the point.

The mill is Libeco. The cotton is Mantero. The hands are Ýr's. The coat is yours.

## 2 — Collection note

*Spring 2027 — A note*

Six pieces this spring. Linen from Belgium, cotton from Italy, wool from Yorkshire. Numbered editions of twenty-four. Made in the studio in Reykjavík over four months.

The spring is a long coat, a soft shirt, a wide trouser, a quiet dress, a hand-knit, and a jacket cut for the last week of warm weather. The names are short because the pieces are themselves; there is no "Athena" or "The Elysian". A linen shirt is a linen shirt.

Edition cards are letterpressed at Kaspersson Tryggvason in Hafnarfjörður. The packaging is a cream dust bag, a single cotton drawstring, and a card that tells you which of the twenty-four you have.

The collection is small on purpose. We made what we could make well. The rest is for next year.

## 4 — Longer bio

Ýr Þrastardóttir was born in Oslo in 1984, the daughter of two graduate students who moved the family to Reykjavík when she was five. She grew up there.

She studied fashion at the Iceland Academy of the Arts and graduated in 2011. Her graduation collection brought press attention and led her, at twenty-six, to start YR Collections — a small fashion label that ran for two years before being folded into Another Creation, which she co-founded in 2013.

Another Creation has shown four times at Reykjavík Fashion Festival. She represented Iceland at Designer's Nest during Copenhagen Fashion Week in 2011. She was selected from over 200 applicants for StartupReykjavík in 2013, and the same year was given a special award at the Creative Business Cup for combining the creative side with the commercial.

In 2024 she completed a BA at the Iceland Film School, in Creative Technology. Her short film *Agnes Iwaz* took the Best Movie award in its category. Since then she has divided her work between the studio and the edit room — designing for film, theatre, the Iceland Dance Company, and Another Creation in equal measure.

She lives in Reykjavík, on Vatnsstígur. She makes dresses and coats to measure. She doesn't post often.

## 6 — Interview

*Document Journal, January 2027.*

The studio is on the second floor of a building on Vatnsstígur, in the centre of Reykjavík. There is a long cutting table, a Berning industrial machine that's been serviced more than it's been used, and a window onto the street. The day is overcast in the way Reykjavík is usually overcast — without weather, almost. She makes coffee.

**DOCUMENT:** You make six pieces a season. What's the editorial decision that gets you to six?

**ÝÞ:** It's not really editorial. It's how much I can cut by hand between September and March without losing the part of cutting I actually enjoy. If we made eight, two of them would be cut by someone else; if we made four, I'd start second-guessing them.

**DOCUMENT:** You came from film school recently. Did that change how you design?

**ÝÞ:** It changed the pace. In film you build something for a year and it exists in two hours. In fashion you build a garment for two months and it exists for a decade. Both are slow in different ways. I like that I get to do both.

**DOCUMENT:** Anything you're not telling us about Spring?

**ÝÞ:** There's a coat that took me longer than it should have. I'm not sure yet if it stays in the collection.

## 9 — Product page description

**Burgundy wool coat**

Heavy boiled wool from a single mill in Yorkshire, lined in cotton from Como. Cut wide enough for a thick knit underneath; long enough to fall just below the knee. One internal button at the chest; the front lies clean.

Made by hand in Reykjavík over six weeks. Edition of twenty-four. Each piece is numbered and signed on the inner seam at the right side. Ships in a cream dust bag with a letterpressed edition card.

The coat will soften with wear. Boiled wool is slow to break in but, once it has, holds its shape for years. Spot-clean only; if the coat needs more, send it back and we'll see to it.

## Open questions

Things that came up writing these pieces, worth deciding before they harden:

- **Pronouns for the brand.** "We" (the studio) vs. third-person ("the studio", "Another Creation"). The newsletter uses we; the press release uses third-person. Both feel right in context — but should be deliberate.
- **First-person from Ýr.** Used in the interview and implied in the newsletter. Used nowhere on the site. Where, if anywhere, does first-person belong on the site itself?
- **Collaborator credit conventions.** Photographer Sigurður Júlíus, mill names, knitter — credited as flat names with locations. Settle whether this stays editorial (no role labels needed) or whether longer pieces want "Photography: Name" front matter.
- **Cite format for quoted material.** Walter Murch attributed in passing; Slimane quote-tagged "says Þrastardóttir" in the press release. Pick one convention for ongoing use.
- **Tense.** Most of these use present (the studio cuts, the linen arrives). Past tense slips in for biography. Default to present except for biography and historical reference.
- **Localised copy (IS).** None of this is in Icelandic. Out of scope for now.
